# SAIS Farmer Activity Overlay: The Human-Machine Interface Schema

## 1. Introduction

The core SAIS Ontological Map defines the biophysical reality of the regenerative farm — the entities, metrics, and causal relationships that govern the ecosystem. However, a farm is a managed ecosystem. The **Farmer Activity Overlay** places the human actor at the center of this map. 

This overlay defines the human-machine interface (HMI) schema for the SAIS intelligence platform. It maps how a farmer interacts with each of the nine ecological layers through three primary modes of action:
1.  **Observation:** What the farmer looks for (sensory input).
2.  **Decision:** The cognitive process of evaluating observations against goals.
3.  **Intervention:** The physical action taken to alter the ecosystem state.

By mapping these activities, the SAIS intelligence platform can contextualize sensor data against human intent, providing actionable insights rather than just raw data.

## 2. The Observation-Decision-Intervention Cycle

Regenerative agriculture relies heavily on adaptive management. The farmer operates in a continuous loop of observing the land, making decisions based on those observations, and intervening to steer the ecosystem toward a desired state [1].

### 2.1 Observation (The Sensory Input)

Observation is the cornerstone of regenerative management [1]. Before SAIS, this was entirely manual and qualitative. SAIS augments human observation with quantitative, continuous data.

*   **Manual Observations:** Visual assessments of plant health, soil aggregation, presence of wildlife, livestock behavior, and water infiltration during rain events.
*   **SAIS Augmentation:** Continuous monitoring of soil moisture, CO₂ flux, acoustic proxies for livestock rumination, and vision AI for biodiversity tracking.

### 2.2 Decision (The Cognitive Process)

Decisions in regenerative agriculture are context-dependent and dynamic, not prescriptive.

*   **Tactical Decisions (Daily/Weekly):** When to move livestock, whether to terminate a cover crop, adjusting irrigation based on immediate weather and soil moisture.
*   **Strategic Decisions (Seasonal/Annual):** Crop rotation planning, selecting cover crop mixes, determining overall stocking rates, and planning infrastructure investments.

### 2.3 Intervention (The Physical Action)

Interventions are the levers the farmer pulls to influence the causal graph defined in the core ontology.

*   **Biological Interventions:** Applying compost tea, planting diverse cover crops, introducing specific livestock.
*   **Mechanical Interventions:** Roller-crimping, minimal tillage (if necessary), moving temporary fencing.
*   **Chemical Interventions:** Applying targeted foliar nutrition (minimizing synthetic inputs).

| **1. Soil Biology** | Earthworm presence, residue breakdown rate, soil smell. | Is the biological engine active enough to cycle nutrients? | Apply compost extract, adjust grazing impact. | Package 9 (CO₂ flux) indicates real-time microbial respiration. |
| **2. Soil Chemistry** | Plant deficiency symptoms, soil test results. | Are nutrients available in the right forms? | Apply targeted foliar sprays, adjust cover crop mix to fix N or scavenge P. | Package 1 (NPK probe) tracks nutrient availability trends. |
| **3. Soil Physics** | Compaction layers (penetrometer), aggregate stability (slake test). | Is the soil structure limiting root growth or water infiltration? | Deep rip (rarely), plant deep-rooting cover crops (radish). | Package 1 (Moisture/Temp) shows compaction effects on water movement. |
| **4. Plant Health** | Leaf color, pest pressure, Brix levels. | Are plants photosynthesizing optimally and resisting pests? | Apply biostimulants, alter grazing timing. | Manual Brix input + Vision AI for pest/disease detection. |

| **6. Atmosphere** | (Difficult to observe manually) | Is the farm sequestering carbon and minimizing GHG emissions? | Shift to no-till, optimize livestock feed efficiency. | CO₂ flux and acoustic proxies for enteric methane. |
| **7. Livestock** | Body condition score, manure consistency, grazing behavior. | When should the herd be moved to optimize forage and soil impact? | Move temporary fencing (adaptive multi-paddock grazing). | GPS tags track density; acoustic tags monitor rumination. |

## 4. The Intelligence Platform Interface

The Farmer Activity Overlay transforms the SAIS dashboard from a data viewer into a decision-support tool. 

When the system detects an anomaly (e.g., a drop in soil moisture in Layer 3), it doesn't just trigger an alarm. It traverses the causal graph and presents the farmer with:
1.  **The Observation:** "Soil moisture dropping rapidly in Field A."
2.  **The Context:** "Plant canopy cover is low (Layer 4), increasing evaporation (Layer 5)."
3.  **The Suggested Intervention:** "Consider delaying grazing in Field A to allow canopy recovery, or plan for earlier cover crop termination to preserve moisture."

This overlay ensures that the intelligence platform speaks the language of farm management, directly linking biophysical data to human action.
